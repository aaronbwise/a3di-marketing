National averages can tell a misleading story.

When Alive & Thrive needed to understand how maternal and child health and nutrition had changed across Cambodia, Lao PDR, and Viet Nam, the headline numbers showed progress.

But they needed to know: progress for whom?

I built a reproducible Python pipeline to harmonize 15+ rounds of survey data spanning over 20 years — different instruments, different variable names, different coding schemes across countries and time periods. The goal was a single, comparable dataset that could be disaggregated by wealth, ethnicity, geography, and education.

The findings told a different story from the national averages. Progress was deeply uneven. Wealth-driven disparities persisted, and in Lao PDR ethnic and regional gaps exceeded 35 percentage points. Health service access had expanded, but quality of care had barely moved.

A&T used the findings for country strategies, donor advocacy, and knowledge products across the Mekong region.

The takeaway: if your programme is tracking national averages, you might be missing the people who need you most. Disaggregated analysis is how you find them.
